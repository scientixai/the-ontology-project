Eight verification questions for you

  1. Description. Does the description match how you would explain a Sponsor to a ClinOps director who is new to TOP? — accept / rewrite
  2. Hierarchy. Is belongsToOrganization always 1..1, or are there real cases (early-stage Sponsor records, investigator-sponsors before incorporation) where 0..1 is more honest? — 1..1 / 0..1 / split into two cases
  3. parentSponsor. Worth keeping at all, or does the corporate hierarchy fully resolve through Organization.parentOrganization, leaving parentSponsor redundant? — keep / drop / keep with note
  4. Attribute completeness. Twelve attributes. Anything missing that an operator regularly fills out at the sponsor level (primary therapeutic-area focus, employee size band, regulatory authority of record, fiscal year)? Anything that does not belong here at all? — add / drop / accept
  5. Investigator-Sponsor coverage. An academic single-PI sponsor: sponsorType=INVESTIGATOR_SPONSOR, orcid populated, duns empty, legalName possibly = the PI's own name. Does the model survive this case, or does it want a separate object? — survives / needs split / needs hint
  6. CRO-as-Sponsor coverage. A CRO contracted as legal sponsor: sponsorType=CRO_AS_SPONSOR, belongsToOrganization points to the CRO. Is "Sponsor" the right top-level for this case, or should CRO get its own top-level later? — stays under Sponsor / CRO becomes top-level / flag for v0.3
  7. Polysemous verbs. The OOUX map has Sponsor publishes Document and publishes Publication; operates Training Program and operates System. The @context collapses each verb to a single term and lets the relationship instance carry the typed target. Acceptable, or do you want explicit term names (publishesDocument, publishesPublication)? — collapse / split
  8. Calls to Action. Eight CTAs. Operator-grade enough? Anything missing that you have seen sponsor leads do regularly (reassign primary contact, transfer studies between programs, request public listing on ClinicalTrials.gov)? — add / accept

Description and editor note prose summary, top of the entry

A Sponsor is the legal organization responsible for initiating, managing, and financing one or more clinical trials. Sponsors hold regulatory accountability for their studies and own the master data that anchors most other entities.
v0.2 editor note (from OOUX map Section 6): type vocabulary expanded to include CRO_AS_SPONSOR; added duns and orcid identifiers; added regulatory and finance contact relationships rather than embedded fields. Investigator-Sponsor (academic single-PI sponsor) is supported via sponsorType. v0.2-TOP additions: belongsToOrganization and parentSponsor (USDM-aligned hierarchy).

Real-world scenarios does the model survive contact with the hard cases?

Case 1 · Multi-business-unit pharma (J&J) large enterprise
J&J is the corporate parent. J&J Innovative Medicine and J&J MedTech are operating companies. Janssen R&D sits inside J&J Innovative Medicine. A study is sponsored at the Janssen R&D level; regulatory accountability rolls up to J&J Innovative Medicine; financial reporting rolls up to J&J.
Sponsor{name: "Janssen R&D", sponsorType: PHARMACEUTICAL, status: ACTIVE}
  belongsToOrganization → Organization{name: "Janssen R&D", type: SPONSOR, parentOrganization → Organization{name: "J&J Innovative Medicine", type: OPERATING_COMPANY, parentOrganization → Organization{name: "Johnson & Johnson", type: PARENT_CORPORATION}}}
  parentSponsor → (empty; J&J Innovative Medicine is not separately a Sponsor entity in this study)
Does this resolve cleanly, or does the operator want to see "J&J Innovative Medicine" as a Sponsor entity too, with Janssen R&D as its parentSponsor child?
Case 2 · Investigator-sponsor (academic single PI) edge case
Dr. Maria Chen at MD Anderson runs an investigator-initiated trial. She is the legal sponsor. Her institution provides administrative support but is not the sponsor of record.
Sponsor{name: "Maria Chen, MD", sponsorType: INVESTIGATOR_SPONSOR, orcid: "0000-0001-...", duns: empty, status: ACTIVE}
  belongsToOrganization → Organization{name: "Maria Chen, MD (Investigator-Sponsor)", type: SPONSOR, parentOrganization → Organization{name: "MD Anderson Cancer Center", type: ACADEMIC_INSTITUTION}}
  parentSponsor → (empty)
Is it honest to require Dr. Chen to be modeled as her own Organization with a parent? Or does this case need belongsToOrganization to be 0..1 so the Sponsor can stand alone?
Case 3 · CRO acting as legal sponsor contractual edge
A small biotech without internal regulatory capacity contracts a CRO (e.g., IQVIA) to act as the legal sponsor of record for a phase 1 trial. The biotech is the financial sponsor; the CRO carries regulatory accountability.
Sponsor{name: "IQVIA (sponsor of record for Acme Bio Phase 1)", sponsorType: CRO_AS_SPONSOR, status: ACTIVE}
  belongsToOrganization → Organization{name: "IQVIA", type: CRO, parentOrganization: empty}
  parentSponsor → (empty)
  contractsWith → CRO{name: "IQVIA"} ← RECURSIVE / SELF-REFERENCE PROBLEM
When the CRO is the sponsor, the contractsWith → CRO relationship becomes a self-loop. Is this acceptable, or does CRO_AS_SPONSOR need a different relationship pattern? Also: should the financial-sponsor (Acme Bio) appear anywhere in the model, or is that a Contract-side concern?
Case 4 · Government sponsor (NIH-funded trial) public sector
An NCI-funded oncology trial. The sponsor of record is the National Cancer Institute (an NIH institute). The legal hierarchy: NCI sits under NIH, which sits under HHS.
Sponsor{name: "National Cancer Institute", sponsorType: GOVERNMENT, status: ACTIVE}
  belongsToOrganization → Organization{name: "NCI", type: GOVERNMENT, parentOrganization → Organization{name: "NIH", type: GOVERNMENT, parentOrganization → Organization{name: "HHS", type: GOVERNMENT}}}
  parentSponsor → (empty)
Does the GOVERNMENT type code carry enough information, or should we add a sub-type (DEPARTMENT, AGENCY, INSTITUTE, OFFICE) to capture the federal hierarchy more precisely?
