The Ontology Project

Operator-grounded reference knowledge graphs as a commons.
AI as a force multiplier for the people who run regulated industries — not a force reduction agent that replaces them.

There are plenty of ontologies for biomarkers, proteins, gene products, disease taxonomies, and clinical terminologies. They were built for research and reporting. None of them encode what a clinical research coordinator carries in her head when she walks into a site, or what a Sponsor PM clicks on through the day, or what a pharmacist needs to verify before dispensing investigational product. Operators don't benefit from ontologies that don't understand their workflows. TOP is the missing layer: industry-agnostic infrastructure for reference ontologies grounded in the operator's mental model, the foundation that lets AI surface the next thing the operator needs to do, with provenance, instead of replacing the operator entirely. The first reference graph being built on TOP is for clinical research; CMC, drug discovery, regulatory, claims, healthcare delivery, energy and process industries, manufacturing, cell therapy, and rare disease are queued.

The TOP Manifesto

Two market failures interlock today. The first is older: the knowledge structures beneath an organization's data belong to someone else. Data can be exported. Ontologies cannot. The second is newer and acute. In May 2026, Anthropic announced a $1.5B joint venture with Blackstone, Hellman & Friedman, and Goldman Sachs to deliver enterprise AI transformation services. OpenAI matched it the same week with TPG and Bain Capital. Vertical integration of model provider with implementer with deployment relationship now compresses what were previously four dimensions of governance sovereignty (named by Frédéric Verhelst, PhD: semantic, enforcement, access, container) into one counterparty. We name a fifth that the joint-venture pattern makes unavoidable: provenance independence.

"When the firm implementing your agents also provides the model, who owns the meaning your agents act on? If the answer is the vendor, the procurement contract is not what it appears to be. It is a transfer of the operational graph at enterprise scale, executed across private equity portfolios at speed."

Frédéric Verhelst, PhD · public analysis on the joint-venture pattern, May 2026

The compounding gap is that even where ontologies exist for healthcare, they were built by domain scientists for research and reporting (biomarkers, proteins, disease taxonomies, clinical terminologies). They serve their original purpose well. They do not serve the operator. A clinical research coordinator running five active studies, a pharmacist preparing investigational product for tomorrow's visits, a study monitor covering twelve sites: none of them have the foundation they need to deploy AI as augmentation. Without operator-shaped ontology, the AI offered to them is force-reduction (replace the coordinator) rather than force-multiplication (let one coordinator run ten studies instead of two). TOP is the foundation that makes the second choice possible.

We are starting in healthcare and life sciences because the cost there is measured in patients. The architecture transfers.

A short selection of what we commit to:

  • Open licensing. Every reference graph we publish is licensed for reuse, redistribution, and commercial application without permission.
  • Versioned releases. Reference graphs ship with semantic versions, changelogs, and migration notes.
  • Governance with rotation. Stewardship is a role, not a tenure. Domain working groups elect and rotate editors on a published cadence.
  • A door out. Everything we host can be taken and run elsewhere. If we ever become a chokepoint, we have already failed our own test.

How to contribute

TOP is being shaped now, in the open. While working groups form, the contribution surface is small but real. The earliest edits carry the most weight.

1. Argue with the manifesto
v0.2 stands open for endorsement and refinement. If you believe knowledge is the work and data is only what the work enables, read the manifesto and mark it up. Where it is wrong, say so. Where it is vague, push. Founding signatories retain the right to refine the manifesto's language as a condition of signing.
2. Read a reference graph spec and find a gap
The Sponsor spec carries eight closed verification questions and a tracked open-issues list. The Site + StudySite spec carries fourteen verification questions still pending review. Practitioners who have lived with these objects in production are the right reviewers for both. File an issue against any specific decision; the per-spec open-issues table records every concern, named.
3. Propose a working group for an adjacent domain
CMC, drug discovery, regulatory, commercial, claims, healthcare delivery, energy and process industries, manufacturing, cell therapy, rare disease: the architecture transfers. If you carry a reference-graph need in any of these, the right move is to convene the practitioners who would steward it. TOP Core (top:) is governed centrally as the universal layer that every domain inherits; each domain owns its own reference graph (topcr:, topcmc:, etc.) under its own working group, extending Core rather than redeclaring it.
4. Submit an RFC
Amendments arrive as RFCs (markdown documents in governance/rfcs/), get reviewed by the working group, and merge through pull request with at least one approving review from a working-group member. The full process and the RFC template land before the public publish.

For now, while working groups are forming, Bo Lora as convener is the review pool of one. As working groups spin up, governance rotates to those groups, and founding signatories on the manifesto step into advisory roles.
